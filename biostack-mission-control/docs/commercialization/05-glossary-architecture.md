## 1. Strategic Purpose

A well-executed glossary is not a dictionary. It is the foundation of BioStack's search moat — a cluster of topically authoritative pages that rank for high-intent queries, demonstrate expertise to AI answer engines (AEO/GEO), and funnel readers directly to the product features that solve the problems these terms represent.

The search landscape for bio-optimization content is fragmented. Most content either overclaims (supplement marketing) or underclaims (clinical disclaimers that answer nothing). BioStack occupies an unclaimed position: precise, math-grounded, observability-first content that treats the reader as a sophisticated self-experimenter who wants clarity, not caution-washing.

**Three goals every glossary page must serve:**

1. **Rank** — Target terms with real search volume among the BioStack audience. Answer what people are actually searching for.
2. **Convert** — Every definition has a natural bridge to a BioStack feature. The reader should always leave understanding what BioStack does that no spreadsheet can do.
3. **Earn AI citations** — Structure definitions for extraction by LLMs, voice assistants, and AI overviews. Short, precise definitions up front. Supporting context after. Schema markup throughout.

## 5. Definition Format Standard

### The Four-Layer Rule

Every definition must be writable in four layers, from shortest to longest:

1. **Schema layer (under 25 words):** Used in structured data, meta descriptions, and AI answer extraction. Must stand alone as a complete definition.
2. **Callout layer (1–2 sentences):** The bolded quote at the top of the page. Expands on schema layer slightly.
3. **Body layer (2–4 paragraphs):** The full educational treatment with context.
4. **FAQ layer:** Anticipates specific follow-up questions. Each answer must be self-contained.

### Voice and Framing Rules

| Do | Do Not |
|---|---|
| "Researchers working with [compound]..." | "You should..." |
| "When reconstituting a lyophilized peptide..." | "To inject properly..." |
| "Protocol managers track..." | "Users need to..." |
| "BioStack surfaces..." / "BioStack calculates..." | "BioStack tells you to..." |
| "Evidence suggests..." | "Studies prove..." |
| "This compound has been studied for..." | "This compound treats..." |
| "Common in research settings..." | "Safe for use by..." |

### Precision Requirements

- Never use "safe," "effective," or "proven" without immediately attributing to a specific evidence tier and source.
- Dosage-adjacent content must always reference the Reconstitution Calculator rather than state specific doses.
- All mechanism descriptions must attribute to the biological pathway, not to a claimed outcome.
- When a term appears in BioStack's evidence tier system, link to the Evidence Tiers explainer page.

### AEO/GEO Optimization Rules

- Lead every definition with the subject noun (e.g., "Reconstitution is...") not a dependent clause.
- The schema-layer definition must be extractable by LLMs without surrounding context.
- Include "what is [term]" phrasing in at least one H2 per page.
- FAQ blocks must use `Q:` / `A:` format and each A must answer completely without referencing the question.
- Use concrete numbers and units when relevant (e.g., "expressed in micrograms per milliliter" not "expressed in small units").

### Reconstitution

*Category: Calculators & Math | Priority: High Search, High Commercial | Build Priority: 1*

> **Reconstitution is the process of mixing a lyophilized (freeze-dried) powder compound with a sterile liquid diluent to produce a solution at a known concentration. Most research peptides are shipped and stored in lyophilized form and require reconstitution before use.**

---

**What is Reconstitution?**

Reconstitution refers to the process of rehydrating a lyophilized (freeze-dried) compound — most commonly a research peptide — by adding a precise volume of sterile liquid, called a diluent. The result is a solution with a calculable concentration, typically expressed in micrograms per milliliter (mcg/mL).

Lyophilization is used to preserve compound stability during shipping and storage. When a compound is removed from solution and freeze-dried, it becomes a stable powder that can survive at room temperature or in refrigeration without degrading rapidly. Before the compound can be used, it must be reconstituted by adding back a precise volume of diluent.

The two most common diluents used in reconstitution are bacteriostatic water (sterile water preserved with 0.9% benzyl alcohol) and sterile water for injection. The choice of diluent affects stability and shelf life of the resulting solution.

**Why Reconstitution Matters for Protocol Work**

The reconstitution calculation determines the final concentration of the compound — how many micrograms are present in each milliliter of solution. Every subsequent measurement depends on this number. An error in reconstitution math propagates to every administered volume.

For protocol managers working with multiple compounds at different concentrations, tracking reconstitution data across a stack introduces real complexity. Knowing that 5mg of a peptide reconstituted with 2mL of bacteriostatic water produces a concentration of 2,500 mcg/mL is not intuitive math at 6am.

**How BioStack Handles Reconstitution**

BioStack's Reconstitution Calculator automates the complete reconstitution calculation. Users enter the dry weight of their compound (in milligrams or micrograms) and the volume of diluent added (in milliliters). The calculator outputs the exact concentration per mL, per unit volume, and per target dose. It also stores the reconstituted compound data alongside the protocol, so the concentration remains accessible throughout the protocol phase.

**Related BioStack feature:** [Reconstitution Calculator →]

**Related Terms**
- [Lyophilized powder]: The freeze-dried form of a compound that requires reconstitution before use.
- [Bacteriostatic water]: The most common diluent used in peptide reconstitution.
- [Diluent]: Any sterile liquid used to rehydrate a lyophilized compound.
- [Concentration]: The output of reconstitution math — how much compound is present per unit volume.
- [mcg vs mg]: The unit relationship central to reading and applying reconstitution results.

**Frequently Asked Questions**

**Q: What is the difference between reconstitution and dilution?**  
A: Reconstitution restores a lyophilized (dry) compound to liquid form by adding a diluent to it. Dilution is the process of reducing the concentration of an already-liquid solution by adding more solvent. Reconstitution is performed once on a new lyophilized compound; dilution may be performed afterward to achieve a lower working concentration.

**Q: How much bacteriostatic water is typically used to reconstitute a peptide?**  
A: The volume of bacteriostatic water added during reconstitution determines the final concentration. BioStack's Reconstitution Calculator accepts any combination of dry compound weight and diluent volume and computes the resulting concentration — allowing protocol managers to choose a diluent volume that results in a convenient per-dose measurement.

**Q: Does BioStack store reconstitution data for each compound?**  
A: Yes. BioStack saves reconstituted compound data — including dry weight, diluent volume, and calculated concentration — as part of the compound record within the protocol. This means the concentration remains accessible and consistent across check-ins and dose calculations throughout the protocol phase.

### Half-Life

*Category: Pharmacokinetics | Priority: High Search, High Commercial | Build Priority: 2*

> **Half-life is the time required for the concentration of a compound in the body to reduce by 50%. It is a primary pharmacokinetic variable that determines how often a compound must be administered to maintain a target concentration range.**

---

**What is Half-Life?**

Half-life is a pharmacokinetic term describing the time it takes for the active concentration of a compound — whether in blood plasma, tissue, or the whole body — to fall to half its original value. It is typically expressed in hours or minutes, though some long-acting compounds are measured in days.

Half-life is not a fixed biological constant; it can vary based on the route of administration, the form of the compound (free peptide vs. conjugated), the individual's metabolic rate, organ function, and body composition. For peptides specifically, half-life is often short — many unmodified peptides have half-lives measured in minutes — while modified or PEGylated analogs may extend this significantly.

Understanding a compound's half-life is essential for two reasons: determining the frequency of administration that maintains stable tissue levels, and identifying the window after cessation during which the compound remains active.

**Why Half-Life Matters for Protocol Work**

Half-life is a core variable in protocol structure. A compound with a 30-minute half-life used twice daily produces a very different concentration profile than one with a 6-hour half-life dosed once daily. Protocol managers who track multiple compounds simultaneously need to account for the combined activity windows of all compounds — not just each one individually.

Half-life also informs the wash-out period calculation. Before transitioning between protocols or introducing new compounds, understanding how long each current compound remains biologically active prevents unintended overlap periods.

**Frequently Asked Questions**

**Q: What does half-life mean in practice for someone designing a peptide protocol?**  
A: Half-life determines how long after each administration a compound remains at meaningful concentration. A compound with a 2-hour half-life will have reduced to roughly 6% of peak concentration within 8 hours. This directly informs how many administrations per day are needed to sustain a target activity window — and how quickly the compound clears when discontinued.

**Q: Is peptide half-life the same as the duration of effect?**  
A: Not necessarily. Half-life describes the pharmacokinetic clearance rate. Duration of effect may be longer or shorter depending on downstream biological mechanisms the compound activates. Some compounds trigger effects that persist well beyond their own clearance. Half-life is one input into understanding duration of effect, not the complete picture.

### Pathway Overlap

*Category: Protocol Design & Observability | Priority: Low Search / High Commercial | Build Priority: 3*

> **Pathway overlap occurs when two or more compounds in a protocol act on the same biological pathway, receptor, or mechanism — producing combined effects that may differ from either compound administered alone.**

---

**What is Pathway Overlap?**

Pathway overlap is the condition in which multiple compounds within a protocol share a common biological mechanism — whether they target the same receptor, upregulate the same downstream signaling cascade, or modulate the same physiological process. When compounds share pathway activity, their combined effect may be additive (effects sum), synergistic (effects amplify beyond the sum), or antagonistic (effects partially cancel).

Pathway overlap is not inherently beneficial or problematic. The relevance depends on the goal of the protocol, the magnitude of each compound's effect at its pathway, and the sensitivity of the individual running the protocol. The challenge is that pathway overlap is often non-obvious: two compounds with very different names and mechanisms may both converge on, for example, mTOR signaling or VEGF-mediated angiogenesis.

Protocol managers working with multi-compound stacks frequently find that they have inadvertently combined compounds with significant pathway overlap — not from recklessness but from the genuine complexity of cross-referencing mechanism data across multiple literature sources.

**Why Pathway Overlap Matters for Protocol Work**

Untracked pathway overlap creates two categories of problems: first, redundancy that may waste compound; second, unanticipated intensity of effect from unexpected synergy. In both cases, the protocol manager lacks the observability to understand what is driving the results they are seeing — or not seeing.

Tracking pathway overlap also becomes important when designing a wash-out period between protocol phases. If multiple compounds share a pathway and are discontinued at different times, the pathway's activation may persist longer than expected based on any single compound's half-life alone.

**How BioStack Handles Pathway Overlap**

BioStack's Pathway Overlap Detector analyzes all compounds in an active protocol and surfaces potential mechanism overlaps based on the compound knowledge base. Overlaps are flagged by category — additive, synergistic potential, or contraindicated — with links to the supporting evidence tier for each flagged relationship. This allows protocol managers to make informed decisions about compound combinations before beginning a phase, rather than discovering interactions through observation alone.

**Frequently Asked Questions**

**Q: What is the difference between pathway overlap and a drug interaction?**  
A: Drug interaction is a clinical term typically used in a pharmaceutical context to describe adverse or altered medication effects when two drugs are used together. Pathway overlap is a broader, mechanism-focused concept that includes any shared biological signaling route — whether the result is beneficial, neutral, or concerning. BioStack uses pathway overlap terminology to describe the structural relationship between compounds without making clinical interaction claims.

**Q: Can pathway overlap be intentional and desirable?**  
A: Yes. Many protocol managers deliberately select compounds with complementary or overlapping pathways to achieve specific outcomes. For example, BPC-157 and TB-500 are frequently combined in protocols because their mechanisms — while distinct — share angiogenic and tissue repair pathway activity in a way that many researchers consider synergistic. The goal of BioStack's Pathway Overlap Detector is to make this overlap explicit and evidence-linked, not to discourage it.

The Pathway Overlap Detector cross-references these annotations across all compounds in the active protocol and surfaces intersections, filtered by evidence quality.

### Evidence Tier

*Category: Knowledge Base | Priority: Low Search / High Commercial | Build Priority: 3*

> **An evidence tier is a classification label that indicates the quality and strength of the research supporting a stated mechanism, effect, or claim about a compound. BioStack assigns evidence tiers to all knowledge base content to make research quality transparent.**

---

**What is an Evidence Tier?**

An evidence tier is a structured classification system for rating the quality of evidence behind a scientific claim. In the context of bio-protocol management, evidence tiers communicate whether a compound's effects have been demonstrated in rigorous human clinical trials, in animal models, in cell culture (in vitro), or primarily through theoretical mechanism reasoning.

BioStack uses a four-tier evidence framework:

- **Strong:** Replicated human clinical trial data supporting the claimed effect.
- **Moderate:** Human clinical data exists but is limited in scale, duration, or replication; or consistent animal model data with mechanistic plausibility in humans.
- **Limited:** Preliminary animal or in vitro data; early-stage human observations without controlled trials.
- **Mechanistic:** No direct trial evidence for the effect, but the biological mechanism by which it could occur has been identified and described. This tier covers theoretical basis only.

Most research peptides occupy the Limited or Mechanistic tiers for most of their claimed effects. This is not a reason to dismiss them — it reflects the reality that compound research takes time and funding. Evidence tiers communicate where the research actually stands, which is more useful than either blanket validation or blanket dismissal.

**Why Evidence Tiers Matter for Protocol Work**

Protocol managers who rely on forum consensus, vendor marketing, or anecdotal reports to evaluate compounds are operating without visibility into the quality of the information driving their decisions. A compound described in dozens of blog posts as "proven for tissue repair" may have all of that writing trace back to a single rat study — or to no study at all.

Evidence tiers make this visible without requiring protocol managers to read every paper. A Mechanistic rating tells the reader: "The mechanism is understood, but the effect in practice has not been tested at scale." A Strong rating tells the reader: "Multiple controlled human trials support this." The difference matters enormously for how a compound is positioned within a protocol.

**How BioStack Handles Evidence Tiers**

Every entry in BioStack's Knowledge Base is tagged with an evidence tier for each claim it makes about a compound. Users can filter the Knowledge Base by evidence tier to see only Strong or Moderate evidence content, or to specifically review Mechanistic content for theoretical grounding. Evidence tier ratings are reviewed and updated as new research becomes available, and each rating includes a link to the source literature that informed the classification.

**Frequently Asked Questions**

**Q: Does a Mechanistic evidence tier mean a compound does not work?**  
A: No. Mechanistic tier means the mechanism by which a compound could produce an effect has been described in the literature, but controlled trials demonstrating that effect in practice have not been completed or are not yet available at scale. Many compounds are biologically plausible and used in research settings under a Mechanistic classification. The tier communicates the state of available evidence, not a judgment on the compound's utility.

**Q: Why does BioStack show evidence tiers instead of just recommending compounds?**  
A: BioStack is an educational and observational platform, not a prescribing authority. Evidence tiers exist so protocol managers can make informed decisions based on actual research quality rather than marketing claims. Making evidence quality visible is a core product value — it is what separates BioStack from supplement marketing and enables serious self-experimenters to think clearly about their protocols.
