### Category 4: Calculators

---

**Q: What is reconstitution and why does the calculator exist?**

Reconstitution is the process of dissolving a lyophilized (freeze-dried) powder compound into a liquid solution, typically bacteriostatic water, for the purpose of injection. Many peptides and research compounds are shipped in lyophilized powder form for stability, and must be reconstituted before use.

The reconstitution calculation — determining the resulting concentration based on powder mass and diluent volume — is straightforward math, but errors carry real consequences. Miscalculating the concentration leads to incorrect dosing. BioStack's Reconstitution Calculator eliminates that risk by automating the calculation precisely. Enter the compound mass in milligrams and the diluent volume in milliliters; BioStack outputs the concentration in mcg/mL and the injection volume for any target dose you specify.

---

**Q: What is the difference between mg and mcg?**

Milligrams (mg) and micrograms (mcg) are both units of mass in the metric system, but they differ by a factor of one thousand. One milligram equals one thousand micrograms. The distinction is critical in compound dosing because many peptides and research compounds are dosed in micrograms — very small amounts where precision matters significantly.

A compound shipped as a 5mg vial contains 5,000 mcg of active compound. If your target dose is 250 mcg per injection, that vial contains 20 doses. BioStack's Unit Conversion tool handles all conversions between mg, mcg, g, and IU, and the Reconstitution Calculator works across units automatically. Understanding the relationship between these units is fundamental to accurate protocol math.

---

**Q: How does the Reconstitution Calculator work?**

The Reconstitution Calculator takes two inputs: the total mass of lyophilized compound in your vial (entered in milligrams) and the volume of diluent you add (entered in milliliters). From these two values, it calculates the resulting concentration in micrograms per milliliter (mcg/mL).

Once concentration is known, you can enter a target dose in micrograms and BioStack immediately outputs the exact injection volume in milliliters you need to draw to hit that dose. For example: a 5mg vial reconstituted with 2mL of bacteriostatic water produces a concentration of 2,500 mcg/mL. A 250 mcg dose at that concentration requires 0.1mL. The calculator handles all of this instantly, with full unit labeling at every step.

---

**Q: How does the Volume Calculator work?**

The Volume Calculator is a companion tool to the Reconstitution Calculator. Once you know your solution's concentration in mcg/mL, the Volume Calculator computes the injection volume needed for any target dose. Enter your desired dose in micrograms and your concentration in mcg/mL — BioStack outputs the exact volume to draw in milliliters.

This tool is useful when you have a concentration already established (from a previous reconstitution calculation or a pre-mixed solution) and simply need to translate a dose into an injection volume. It can also be used independently of the Reconstitution Calculator for compounds that come pre-mixed or for verifying dose calculations across different concentration scenarios.

---

**Q: What unit conversions does BioStack support?**

BioStack's Unit Conversion tool supports all standard mass conversions used in compound dosing: milligrams (mg) to micrograms (mcg), micrograms to milligrams, grams (g) to milligrams and micrograms, and International Units (IU) for applicable compounds. The converter handles bidirectional conversions and displays results with enough decimal precision to be useful for dosing math.

Conversion between IU and mass units (mg or mcg) is compound-specific, as the IU-to-mass ratio differs by substance. BioStack handles this where the conversion is standardized. For compounds where IU conversion is not universally defined, the tool surfaces the appropriate reference values.

**Q: What are evidence tiers and what do they mean?**

Evidence tiers are BioStack's classification system for how well a compound's effects are supported by scientific literature. There are four tiers, applied to each claimed mechanism or effect in a Knowledge Base entry.

**Strong** — Supported by multiple well-designed human clinical trials with consistent, replicated results. **Moderate** — Supported by human trials or robust animal research, with some inconsistency or limited sample sizes. **Limited** — Early-stage evidence, small studies, or preliminary findings; directionally suggestive but not conclusive. **Mechanistic** — Biologically plausible based on known mechanisms, but without direct trial evidence to confirm the effect in humans.

Evidence tiers are not value judgments about whether to use a compound — they are calibration tools for reading the evidence accurately.

---

**Q: What is a synergy flag?**

A synergy flag in the BioStack Knowledge Base indicates that two compounds have documented or mechanistically plausible interactions that may amplify or complement each other's effects. When a compound entry carries a synergy flag for another compound, BioStack surfaces this in the Knowledge Base entry and in the Pathway Overlap Checker when both compounds are present in your stack.

Synergy flags are informational — they tell you that a relationship exists in the literature or mechanism landscape. They are not endorsements, recommendations, or instructions. The flag surfaces information for your awareness; what you do with that information is your decision, ideally made in consultation with a qualified professional.

---

**Q: What are drug interaction flags in the Knowledge Base?**

Drug interaction flags identify where a compound has documented or potentially significant interactions with pharmaceutical drugs or other compounds. These flags are drawn from clinical literature, pharmacokinetic data, and mechanism analysis. When an interaction flag is present, BioStack displays the nature of the interaction and the evidence classification.

Interaction flags are among the most important informational outputs in BioStack. If you are taking any prescription medications alongside a compound protocol, reviewing the interaction flags for every compound in your stack — and sharing that information with your prescribing physician — is a critical safety step. BioStack surfaces this information explicitly so it cannot be overlooked.

**Q: How is Knowledge Base information sourced?**

Knowledge Base entries are compiled from peer-reviewed scientific literature, including human clinical trials, animal studies, and mechanistic research published in indexed journals. The evidence tier classification reflects the volume, quality, and consistency of available evidence for each specific claim. BioStack's editorial process prioritizes accuracy and calibration — entries are written to represent the evidence as it actually exists, not as users might hope it does.

BioStack does not manufacture, invent, or extrapolate beyond what the literature supports. Where evidence is weak or conflicting, that is reflected in the evidence tier (Limited or Mechanistic). ### Category 10: Advanced Features

---

**Q: What is Pathway Overlap detection?**

Pathway Overlap detection is a BioStack feature that analyzes your active compound stack and identifies when two or more compounds share membership in the same biological pathway. Pathways — such as the growth hormone axis, IGF-1 signaling, mTOR activation, or mitochondrial biogenesis — represent the biological mechanisms through which compounds exert their effects. When multiple compounds in your stack work through the same pathway, the Pathway Overlap Checker surfaces that relationship explicitly.

Understanding pathway overlaps is important for several reasons. Compounds that share pathways may produce additive effects, increase the risk of pathway-specific side effects, or compete for receptor binding. The Pathway Overlap Checker does not tell you what to do with that information — it ensures you have it.

---

**Q: What are interaction flags?**

Interaction flags are markers in BioStack's Knowledge Base and Pathway Overlap Checker that indicate a documented or mechanistically significant relationship between two compounds. They appear in two contexts: in individual Knowledge Base entries (flagging interactions between that compound and specific drugs or other compounds) and in the Pathway Overlap results (flagging interactions between compounds currently in your active stack).

Flags are classified by type — synergy, competition, pharmacokinetic interaction, or caution — and by evidence tier. A synergy flag with Strong evidence means the interaction is well-documented in human trials. A caution flag with Mechanistic evidence means the concern is theoretically grounded but not yet confirmed in direct human research. The flag type and evidence tier together give you the context to read the flag accurately.

---

**Q: What is an evidence confidence score?**

Evidence confidence is BioStack's summary assessment of how well the overall body of evidence supports a compound's core claimed effects. It synthesizes the evidence tier ratings across all documented mechanisms for that compound into a single directional indicator: High, Moderate, or Low confidence.

High confidence does not mean a compound is safe or effective for your individual situation — it means the scientific literature is consistent and well-powered in supporting the effect. Low confidence does not mean a compound does not work — it means the evidence base is thin, mixed, or primarily preclinical. Evidence confidence is a calibration tool, not a verdict.

---

**Q: How does BioStack handle compounds that share biological pathways?**

When two or more compounds in your active stack share a biological pathway, BioStack's Pathway Overlap Checker surfaces the shared pathway, identifies both compounds as members, and displays any associated interaction flags between them. The output is a clear, structured view of which pathway is shared, which compounds are involved, the nature of any documented interaction, and the evidence tier for that interaction.

BioStack does not make recommendations about what to do when pathway overlaps are detected. It surfaces the information clearly and completely, so that you have the full picture before making any decisions — ideally in consultation with a qualified professional who can interpret the overlap in the context of your individual health situation and protocol goals.
